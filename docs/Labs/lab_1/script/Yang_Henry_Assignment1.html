Assignment 1: Census Data Quality for Policy Decisions

Evaluating Data Reliability for Algorithmic Decision-Making

Author

Henry Yang

Published

September 24, 2025

Assignment Overview

Scenario

You are a data analyst for the Alaska Department of Human Services. The department is considering implementing an algorithmic system to identify communities that should receive priority for social service funding and outreach programs. Your supervisor has asked you to evaluate the quality and reliability of available census data to inform this decision.

Drawing on our Week 2 discussion of algorithmic bias, you need to assess not just what the data shows, but how reliable it is and what communities might be affected by data quality issues.

Learning Objectives

  • Apply dplyr functions to real census data for policy analysis
  • Evaluate data quality using margins of error
  • Connect technical analysis to algorithmic decision-making
  • Identify potential equity implications of data reliability issues
  • Create professional documentation for policy stakeholders

Part 5: Policy Recommendations

5.1 Analysis Integration and Professional Summary

Your Task: Write an executive summary that integrates findings from all four analyses.

Executive Summary Requirements:

  1. Overall Pattern Identification: What are the systematic patterns across all your analyses?

  2. Equity Assessment: Which communities face the greatest risk of algorithmic bias based on your findings?

  3. Root Cause Analysis: What underlying factors drive both data quality issues and bias risk?

  4. Strategic Recommendations: What should the Department implement to address these systematic issues?

Executive Summary:

Overall Pattern Identification Across Alaska counties, more than half (53.3%) of median household income estimates fall into the “Low Confidence” category, while only 10% qualify as “High Confidence.” The top 3 counties with the highest MOE percentages, such as Dillingham, Yakutat, and Haines, show margins of error above 20%. This indicates systematic reliability challenges in smaller and remote communities. At the tract level, Anchorage demonstrates greater diversity and larger populations, while North Slope shows both lower White representation (32.6%) and smaller sample sizes, reinforcing the vulnerability of rural areas to unreliable data.

Equity Assessment Communities most at risk of algorithmic bias are those in rural and remote counties. For example, Dillingham and Yakutat would be poorly served by algorithms relying on median income, as their estimates carry uncertainty levels of 30–40%. At the tract level, unreliable tracts (74 out of 86) tend to have slightly smaller populations and lower White percentages compared to reliable tracts, suggesting that diverse or minority-heavy communities face greater risks of data-driven misrepresentation.

Root Cause Analysis ACS sample sizes are smaller in rural and low-population tracts, which inflates MOE and decreases estimate stability. This might because Geographic isolation compounds these problems by making data collection more difficult. This indicates that data quality problems are not randomly distributed, but systematically concentrated in communities that are already more vulnerable.

Strategic Recommendations The Department should prioritize three measures to strengthen equity and reliability in data-driven policy. First, apply MOE thresholds in all algorithmic workflows to flag unstable estimates before use. Second, conduct equity audits to ensure algorithms do not misallocate resources to rural or minority communities. Third, communicate uncertainty transparently so policymakers understand the limitations of ACS data in Alaska’s rural and remote contexts.

Technical Notes

Data Sources:

  • U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey 2018-2022 5-Year Estimates.
  • Retrieved via tidycensus R package on 09-24-2025.

Reproducibility:

  • All analysis conducted in R version 2025.05.1+513.
  • Census API key required for replication.
  • Complete code and documentation available at: https://musa-5080-fall-2025.github.io/portfolio-setup-henryyzh1/.

Methodology Notes:

  • Analysis is based on the 2022 ACS 5-year estimates for B19013_001, B01003_001 in county level, B03002_003, B03002_004, B03002_012, B03002_001 in census tract level.
  • Analysis is based on R packages like tidycensus, tidyverse, knitr.
  • Reliability categories were defined by MOE percentage: High (<5%), Moderate (5–10%), and Low (>10%).
  • County selection is based on reliability category and total population.

Limitations:

  • Sample size constraints: There are many rural counties in Alaska have small populations, leading to inflated MOEs and unstable estimates.
  • Temporal scope: Results are based on a single year (2022); year-to-year variability is not captured.
  • Applicability: The MOE thresholds provide a general framework, but tolerance for uncertainty may differ depending on policy area.
